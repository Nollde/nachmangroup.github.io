Expand Your Horizons (EYH)
Spring 2010, 2011 and 2012

From the National EYH website: "Expanding Your Horizons in Science and Mathematics (TM) conferences nurture girls' interest in science and math courses to encourage them to consider careers in science, technology, engineering, and math". Every year, Cornell hosts an EYH conference for middle school girls. As a sophomore, I volunteered with the Society of Physics Students session. Then, as a junior, with senior Tia Pluatz we organized the session (bridge building). The picture below shows Adam Dishaw and I at the session testing the bridges to see how much weight they could hold. My senior year, we had a booth full of demonstrations instead of a session.

Nanodays
Spring 2010, Spring 2011

From the National Website on Nanodays: "NanoDays is a nationwide festival of educational programs about nanoscale science and engineering and its potential impact on the future". The first year I participated (2010), Nanodays was held at the Ithaca Science Center. I helped with a thin film interference demo (bookmarks with nail polish and water) run by the Cornell Institute for Physics Teachers (CIPT) booth. The second year (2011), Nanodays was a two-day event. The first day was the Science Center (where I helped with the thin film booth again) and then the second day was at Cornell's Wilson Synchrotron Laboratory. In addition to manning the spark chamber booth for a long time (this is my favorite demo of all time - see below). I also helped lead tours around the lab.


Northern Light Learning Center
Spring 2011
The Northern Lights Learning Center is a Homeschool Co-op in Ithaca, New York. I was contacted by one of their leaders who asked us if we (the Society of Physics Students) could help them with their physics lessons. Upon agreeing, I worked with the Cornell Center for Materials Research outreach team to design four lesson plans. With about seven volunteer undergraduate students, we went once a week for four weeks to deliver our lessons to a dozen students aged 10-12. One of the parents in the co-op provided transportation for our students to the event. Our four lessons were:
  1. Catapult design; data collection, measurement, prediction, testing hypothesis, some mechanics principles. This was a two week activity.
  2. Electromagnets; electromagnetic phenomena via a hands on experience.
  3. Light phenomena; color mixing, light as a wave, diffraction and spectroscopy. We brought with us tubes of various elements that we passed a current through. We were able to give everyone diffraction grated glasses so they could see the lines associated with the various elements.
  4. Tour of Wilson Laboratory. For the fifth week, we organized and helped out with a tour for the kids and their parents of the accelerator laboratory at Cornell, the Wilson Synchrotron Laboratory (see picture below).

Tutoring at Belle Sherman Elementary School
Fall 2011

Since I first arrived at Cornell, I tried to get involved in outreach with the math department. Every semester this never seemed to work out with my schedule. However, in the fall of 2011, I had the opportunity to tutor math (and whatever else the kids needed help with) with elementary school kids at Belle Sherman Elementary School. Most of the kids I worked with were first generation Americans (from southeast Asia). It was very humbling to try to teach math to kids who are just learning multiplication - I had forgotten what it was like not to know the basics. One of the coolest things about this was that at the end of the semester, I was invited by the kids to their end of the year party. Instead of having a pizza or ice cream party, there was a noodles party.

Cortland YWCA
February 2012

In the winter of 2012, four other students and I braved a snow storm to drive to Courtland (about 30 minutes from Ithaca) to bring some demonstrations to a group of kids at the YWCA. The theme was polymers. In the picture below I am doing an activity where we make silly putty by mixing borax and glue.


Ithaca Festival
June 2012

Every year there is a multiday festival in downtown Ithaca, NY in the first part of the summer. In 2012, I volunteered with the Physics Department booth (I provided transportation on both days and also helped staff the booth). We had many demonstrations and even though it was a bit rainy, we had many adults and children come through to learn about physics. My favorite demo is the bicycle wheel and conservation of angular momentum. This is the demo that got me hooked on physics so I always like to tell others about it.
